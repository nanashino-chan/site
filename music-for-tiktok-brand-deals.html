Quick Answer: What Music License Do TikTok Brand Deals Need?

TikTok brand deals often need music that covers both commercial use and sync use.

That is because sponsored TikTok videos usually promote a brand, product, service, app, campaign, sponsor, or client, and the music is combined with video or visual media.

  • Commercial license may be needed because the video supports a brand, product, service, app, sponsor, campaign, or client.
  • Sync license may be needed because the music is combined with video, captions, creator footage, product shots, AI visuals, or edited social content.
  • Client-use permission may be needed when a creator, UGC producer, editor, or agency delivers the video to a brand.
  • Paid advertising coverage may be needed when the video is used in TikTok Ads, Spark Ads, whitelisted ads, boosted posts, or multi-platform paid social.
  • Documentation matters because brands, agencies, platforms, and media buyers may ask for proof of music rights.

What Are TikTok Brand Deals?

TikTok brand deals are sponsored or commercial creator videos made for brands, products, apps, services, campaigns, agencies, or clients.

They can look casual and organic, but they are often commercial because the content supports brand awareness, product discovery, customer acquisition, app installs, sales, lead generation, or campaign performance.

Because these videos often combine music, creator footage, captions, product shots, and brand messaging, both commercial licensing and sync licensing should be reviewed.

TikTok Sounds vs Licensed Music for Brand Deals

TikTok sounds and in-app music can be useful for organic creator posts, but platform sound availability is not the same as a direct license for sponsored content, paid ads, brand campaigns, or client delivery.

A sound may appear inside TikTok, but that does not automatically mean it is cleared for every business account, TikTok ad, Spark Ad, external edit, influencer campaign, brand reuse, or multi-platform distribution.

Why Famous Songs and Trending Sounds Can Be Risky for TikTok Brand Deals

Famous songs and trending sounds can make TikTok content feel native, but they can be risky when the video is used for sponsored content, TikTok Ads, Spark Ads, client delivery, or brand campaigns.

A single popular song can involve multiple rights holders, including record labels, master owners, publishers, songwriters, composers, distributors, and collecting societies. If a brand uses the original recording in a TikTok brand deal video, the project may involve master rights, composition rights, sync rights, and commercial rights.

Unauthorized use of famous songs in promotional videos has led to real lawsuits and substantial damages. For example, Beastie Boys sued Monster Energy over the use of their songs in a promotional video, and a jury awarded $1.7 million.

This does not mean every unauthorized use will lead to the same result. However, it shows why famous songs can carry serious legal and financial risk when used in advertising, promotion, branded content, or business videos without permission.
