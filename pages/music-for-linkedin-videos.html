Music for LinkedIn Videos

LinkedIn videos are different from casual social media posts. They often represent a company, founder, product, service, employer brand, B2B campaign, or professional point of view.

That makes music licensing important. A LinkedIn video may start as a simple business post, then become a sales asset, product demo, website embed, YouTube upload, paid LinkedIn ad, or campaign cutdown.

This guide explains how B2B marketers, SaaS teams, agencies, founders, creators, and brand teams should choose and document music for LinkedIn videos.

Quick Answer: What Music Can You Use in LinkedIn Videos?

Use music with clear commercial rights when the LinkedIn video supports a business, brand, product, service, hiring effort, B2B campaign, or paid promotion.

LinkedIn videos are usually uploaded as finished business assets. If the music was taken from another platform, a personal music library, a casual creator context, or an unclear free source, it may not match the actual business use. The safer approach is to use music licensed for commercial video use and keep proof of license.

  • • LinkedIn videos often count as business or commercial content.
  • • B2B marketing videos should use music with clear commercial rights.
  • • SaaS demos, product videos, and company updates may need documentation.
  • • Paid LinkedIn ads usually need sync or campaign-specific licensing.
  • • Agencies and clients may ask for proof of music permission.
  • • Keep the receipt, invoice, license certificate, track details, and project records together.

Why LinkedIn Video Music Matters for B2B Marketing

LinkedIn is a professional context. Viewers may be prospects, customers, investors, founders, hiring managers, agencies, partners, or decision-makers. The music should support trust and clarity, not distract from the message.

For B2B marketing, music is part of the brand signal. It can make a product demo feel more polished, a founder message feel more confident, a company update feel more intentional, or a campaign video feel more complete.

The best LinkedIn video music is professional enough for business, calm enough for focus, and licensed clearly enough for marketing use.

What Makes Good Music for LinkedIn Videos?

LinkedIn video music should support professionalism, clarity, and viewer retention. It should not sound too aggressive, too sentimental, too distracting, or too casual for a business environment.

Lo-fi, chillhop, soft electronic, ambient beats, and minimal instrumental tracks can work well because they create a polished background while leaving space for captions, voiceover, product screens, and business messaging.

Professional Tone

The music should feel polished and credible without sounding generic or overly corporate.

Low Distraction

LinkedIn viewers often watch for information, so music should not compete with captions, voiceover, or product details.

B2B Fit

A calm instrumental background works well for SaaS, product marketing, founder content, and business storytelling.

Why Both Master Rights and Copyright Permission Matter

LinkedIn videos use a finished recording as part of a business communication. Depending on the music source, commercial use can involve rights connected to the master recording and rights connected to the underlying musical work.

This matters because a LinkedIn video can move beyond one post. It may be reused on YouTube, embedded on a website, sent in a sales email, included in a pitch deck, adapted into a paid ad, or delivered to a client.

Master Recording

Permission related to the actual recorded sound file used in the LinkedIn video.

Composition Rights

Permission related to the underlying musical work, melody, composition, and copyright.

Usage Scope

Permission should match the actual use: organic LinkedIn post, business video, client asset, or paid campaign.

Commercial Creator License vs Sync License for LinkedIn Videos

The right license depends on whether the LinkedIn video is organic business content, client work, product marketing, or paid advertising.

For organic LinkedIn posts, B2B videos, product demos, company updates, founder videos, and business content, a Commercial Creator License may be appropriate if the use fits the scope. For paid LinkedIn ads, sponsored campaigns, retargeting videos, lead generation ads, and time-limited campaign use, a Sync License or custom campaign license is usually safer.

What Can Go Wrong With Unclear Music in LinkedIn Videos?

Unclear music rights can create problems for teams that treat LinkedIn videos as reusable business assets. A video may be fine as a draft, but become risky when it is reposted, boosted, embedded, or delivered to a client.

  • • A client may ask for proof of music permission after delivery.
  • • A paid LinkedIn campaign may need different rights than an organic post.
  • • A website embed may not be covered by music taken from another platform.
  • • A sales deck or email campaign may reuse the same video outside LinkedIn.
  • • An agency may need licensing documentation for client records.
  • • A founder or company may not know whether the music is reusable in future videos.
  • • A campaign may need emergency music replacement before launch.
  • • A video may be cut into multiple formats without reviewing the license scope.

For LinkedIn videos, music licensing is not just about the upload. It is about the business workflow around that video.

LinkedIn Video Music License Documentation

LinkedIn videos often involve founders, marketers, editors, designers, agencies, sales teams, recruiters, and clients. Keeping a simple music documentation record makes future reuse easier to review.

Good documentation does not guarantee platform behavior, but it creates a clearer record of what music was used, where it was used, and what permission was obtained.

  • • License file or license terms
  • • Receipt or invoice
  • • License certificate PDF if available
  • • Track title and artist name
  • • LinkedIn video title or post URL
  • • Company, brand, client, or campaign name
  • • Platform list where the video will appear
  • • Website, YouTube, sales deck, or paid ad reuse notes
  • • Campaign period or posting dates
  • • Notes about organic, boosted, paid, or reused content

Frequently Asked Questions

Do LinkedIn videos need licensed music?

LinkedIn videos usually need music with clear commercial rights when they are used for B2B marketing, company updates, brand content, product demos, recruiting, paid ads, or client-facing business content.

Can I use any background music in LinkedIn videos?

No. Music used in LinkedIn videos should match the business purpose of the content. A song that is available on another platform, in a personal library, or in a casual creator context may not be cleared for LinkedIn business videos, paid ads, or B2B marketing.

What kind of music works best for LinkedIn videos?

LinkedIn videos usually work best with professional, low-distraction instrumental music. Lo-fi, chillhop, soft electronic, ambient, and minimal beats can support thought leadership, SaaS demos, product explanations, and brand storytelling without overpowering the message.

Is a LinkedIn company video commercial use?

In practical licensing terms, a LinkedIn company video is often commercial or business use because it supports a company, product, service, employer brand, founder profile, campaign, or professional marketing objective.

What music license is best for LinkedIn marketing videos?

For organic LinkedIn posts, B2B videos, product demos, and company content, a Commercial Creator License may be appropriate if the use fits the license scope. For paid LinkedIn ads, sponsored campaigns, or time-limited advertising, a Sync License or custom campaign license is usually safer.

Can one music license cover LinkedIn, YouTube, websites, and sales videos?

Only if the license scope includes those uses. A B2B video may appear on LinkedIn, YouTube, a website, landing page, sales deck, email campaign, and paid ad, so the license should match the full distribution plan.

Can agencies use licensed music in client LinkedIn videos?

Yes, if the license scope allows agency and client work. Agencies should keep documentation showing the license, receipt or invoice, track information, project name, client use, platform list, and campaign scope.

What proof should businesses keep for LinkedIn video music?

Businesses should keep the license file, receipt or invoice, license certificate if available, track title, artist name, project name, LinkedIn post URL, platform list, campaign dates, and notes about permitted or excluded use.
