Can I Use Music on YouTube?

Yes, you can use music on YouTube when you have the correct permission for your video. That permission may come from your own original music, YouTube Audio Library, a commercial music license, or direct approval from the rights holder.

You usually cannot use any song just because you bought it, streamed it, downloaded it, found it online, or gave credit. Music rights depend on the track, license terms, platform rules, and whether the video is monetized, sponsored, commercial, or client-facing.

This guide explains what music you can use on YouTube, what to avoid, how Content ID affects videos, and when a commercial music license is the safer choice.

Direct Answer: What Music Can You Use on YouTube?

You can use music on YouTube when the music is allowed for your exact use. The permission should match your video type, platform, monetization status, commercial purpose, and distribution plan.

  • • Music you created yourself and fully control
  • • Music from YouTube Audio Library, used under its terms
  • • Music with a valid creator or commercial license
  • • Music where you have permission from the rights holder
  • • Public domain music only after checking both composition and recording rights

If you are uploading monetized videos, Shorts, livestreams, podcasts, sponsored videos, ads, brand content, or client work, check the license carefully before publishing.

Why YouTube Music Rules Are Confusing

Many creators confuse access with permission. Just because you can hear, buy, download, or share a song does not mean you can upload it in a YouTube video.

“I bought the song.”

Buying music for listening does not usually grant YouTube upload, monetization, or commercial video rights.

“I gave credit.”

Credit may be required by some licenses, but attribution alone usually does not replace permission.

“It is only a short clip.”

Short uses can still trigger Content ID or copyright issues, especially with popular songs or commercial recordings.

“Royalty-free means copyright-free.”

Royalty-free music can still be copyrighted and may include restrictions, attribution, or platform limits.

What Happens If You Use Music Without Permission?

If you use music without proper permission, YouTube may detect it through Content ID or a rights holder may take action. The result depends on the rights holder’s policy and the type of claim.

  • • A Content ID claim may appear
  • • Monetization may be redirected or limited
  • • Ads may run on your video
  • • The video may be blocked in some regions
  • • Audio may need to be muted, replaced, or removed
  • • A copyright removal request may lead to a copyright strike

For more detail, see: What Happens If You Use Copyrighted Music?

Can You Use YouTube Audio Library Music?

Yes, YouTube Audio Library can be a useful option for creators. However, you should still check each track’s license type, attribution requirement, and usage conditions inside YouTube Studio.

YouTube Audio Library is often useful for standard YouTube creator videos. For sponsored content, client work, paid ads, brand campaigns, or cross-platform commercial use, a dedicated commercial music license may provide clearer rights.

  • • Check whether attribution is required
  • • Save the track name and license information
  • • Confirm whether the use is YouTube-only or multi-platform
  • • Use a commercial license for brand campaigns and client work

For a full comparison, see: YouTube Audio Library vs Commercial Music.

Can You Use Music in YouTube Shorts?

YouTube Shorts may offer music features inside the YouTube product, but music availability and rights can depend on region, catalog agreements, account type, and how the Short is created.

If you upload your own edited Short with external music, or if the Short is part of a brand campaign, sponsored video, product promotion, or business channel, check the license carefully.

  • • In-app Shorts music access may differ from uploaded external music
  • • Brand and business use may require stronger rights clarity
  • • Repurposing Shorts to TikTok, Instagram, or ads may require additional permission
  • • Commercial workflows should use music with clear license scope

Can You Use Music in Monetized YouTube Videos?

Yes, but only if the music license allows monetized YouTube use. Monetization makes music usage more important because a claim may redirect revenue or limit your ability to earn from the video.

If your channel is part of a business, includes sponsors, uses affiliate links, promotes products, or produces videos for clients, use music with commercial-use permission and keep proof of license.

  • • Check YouTube monetization permission
  • • Check Content ID policy
  • • Check commercial-use terms
  • • Check sponsored-content permission
  • • Check client-work or agency-use terms
  • • Save proof of license before uploading

FAQ

Can I use music on YouTube?

Yes, if you have the correct permission for your video. This may include music you created yourself, music from YouTube Audio Library used under its terms, properly licensed music, or music where you have permission from the rights holder.

Can I use copyrighted music on YouTube?

You generally need permission from the rights holder to use copyrighted music on YouTube. Without permission, the video may receive a Content ID claim, lose monetization, be blocked, or receive a copyright strike in more serious cases.

Can I use music on YouTube if I give credit?

Credit alone usually does not replace permission. Some licenses require attribution, but attribution does not automatically grant the right to use copyrighted music.

Can I use Spotify or Apple Music songs on YouTube?

Streaming or purchasing music for listening generally does not grant permission to upload, monetize, or commercially use that music in YouTube videos.

Can I use music in monetized YouTube videos?

Yes, if the music license clearly allows monetized YouTube use. Monetized videos, sponsored videos, ads, and client work may need a commercial music license.

Can licensed music still get a Content ID claim?

Yes. Some licensed music may still be registered with Content ID. If a claim appears, creators may need to follow the license holder's release process or provide proof of permission.
